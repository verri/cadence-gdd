# Mastery System

Chain Strikes and spells deepen through use, following the *Legend of Dragoon* model, but with a critical constraint: **mastery does not increase damage numbers**. Instead, mastery unlocks **execution options** — more ways to use the move, not a bigger number when you use it.

## Chain Strike Mastery

Each Chain Strike has **3 mastery levels**, earned by completing the Chain successfully N times (exact counts TBD — see [Open Questions](../open-questions.md)).

### Level 1 — Learned

The base Chain with its printed hits, damage, and HP cost. This is what the player gets when they find the Chain Strike in the world. It is fully functional — mastery is not required to use the move effectively.

### Level 2 — Practiced

**Branch inputs unlock.** At specific hits in the Chain, a different button input leads to a variant finisher. The player chooses mid-combo which path to take.

Examples:
- [Ren's](../characters/ren.md) *Ironwake* at Level 2 unlocks a knockback branch on hit 5 alongside the existing damage branch. The player picks based on whether they want raw damage or positional control.
- [Kael's](../characters/kael.md) *Flicker Chain* at Level 2 adds a branch that sacrifices the final hit's damage to inflict a short debuff.
- [Maren's](../characters/maren.md) *Harmonic Drive* at Level 2 lets the player choose between granting Inspired to the next ally OR granting a small Active HP heal to a low-HP ally.

Branch inputs increase the move's **tactical flexibility** without changing its damage output.

### Level 3 — Mastered

**Timing windows tighten, but a new Perfect+ tier appears.** The base Perfect window shrinks, making the Chain harder to execute at maximum grade. In exchange, hitting Perfect+ on the tighter window adds a **bonus effect** unique to that Chain:

- Status application (Bleed, Burnout, etc.)
- An extra hit (extending the Chain by one without increasing HP cost)
- Active HP refund on the finisher
- A buff applied to self or an ally

Base damage is unchanged. The reward for mastery is a higher skill ceiling with richer payoffs — not a flat power increase. A player who can't hit Perfect+ consistently is not penalized; they still have the same Chain they've been using since Level 1.

## Spell Mastery

Each spell has **2 mastery levels**, earned by casting the spell successfully N times.

### Level 1 — Learned

The base spell with its printed rhythm pattern, damage, and MP cost. Fully functional at acquisition.

### Level 2 — Mastered

The rhythm pattern becomes **more complex** — more notes, faster tempo, trickier timing. An S-rank cast on the mastered pattern triggers a **bonus effect** unique to that spell:

- A secondary status condition applied to the target
- An extra damage tick
- A party-wide buff (e.g., a mastered heal spell grants Inspired to the healed character)
- Extended duration on buff/debuff spells

Base damage and MP cost are unchanged. The mastered pattern is harder to S-rank, but the S-rank reward is richer. Players who can't consistently S-rank the harder pattern can still cast the spell at A/B grade with normal results — the floor doesn't drop, only the ceiling rises.

## Why Mastery Matters Without Power Scaling

A mastered *Ironwake* is not "stronger" than a fresh one. It is "more flexible" — the player can choose between a damage finisher, a knockback finisher, or a tight-window bonus effect finisher. A mastered *Spark* spell is not "more damaging" than a fresh one — it has a harder rhythm pattern, but an S-rank cast on the harder pattern also applies a burn status.

The player's **skill ceiling** rises, but the **power floor** stays the same. This is the progression loop: find a move → use it → unlock more ways to use it → find the next move.

## Mastery and the Flat Curve

Mastery is the primary form of "getting better" that the player feels throughout the game. Because stat scaling is nearly flat ([Progression Overview](overview.md)), the sense of growth comes from:
1. Having more Chain Strikes and spells to choose from (horizontal breadth)
2. Having more ways to use each one (mastery depth)
3. The player's own improving execution skill (real-world practice)

All three are forms of progression that reward *engagement* over *time spent*.
